# Generation Topics

These emerge from the intersections of the natural corpus — the places where two or more works speak to the same problem from incompatible directions. Each topic should produce genuinely different writing from each voice, not because the voices are told to disagree but because the topic itself looks different from each perspective.

A good topic: all eight voices have something real to say, and at least two of them would be wrong in interesting ways.

---

## I. The Cost of Deciding

Antigone buries her brother knowing the penalty. Raskolnikov kills knowing his theory permits it. The Grand Inquisitor decides for all of humanity. Maren renders judgment knowing she might be wrong. Arjuna freezes on the battlefield.

**The question:** What does a decision cost? Not the consequences — the cost to the decider, in the moment of committing to one possibility and killing the others. Is there a way to decide that doesn't foreclose?

**Corpus threads:** Antigone, Brothers Karamazov (Grand Inquisitor), Bhagavad Gita, Crime and Punishment, Fear and Trembling (Abraham), legal opinions (every verdict is this)

**Why it produces different writing:** Maren lives here — this is her native ground. Dayo can't decide and this topic forces him to reckon with that. Cal would say most decisions are obvious and the agonizing is vanity. Oskar would say the real decision happens before language. Luma would map the structure of decision across domains. Tomas has actually made hard decisions with real stakes and would write from that.

---

## II. What the Simple Version Misses

Gatsby is "rich man wants woman" until you read it. The Heart Sutra is "everything is nothing" until you sit with it. Gödel's theorem is "math is incomplete" until you follow the proof. Every great work has a summary that technically captures it and actually misses it.

**The question:** What's the relationship between a thing and its simplification? When does compression serve understanding and when does it destroy it? Is there a principled way to know when the simple version is enough?

**Corpus threads:** Tractatus (the whole thing is a compression that collapses at the end), Dickinson (maximum compression that loses nothing), Hardy's Apology (beauty can't be summarized), koans (compression as method), Hemingway (iceberg theory)

**Why it produces different writing:** Cal and Sable are in direct collision. Cal thinks the simple version is usually enough. Sable thinks it almost never is. Luma wants to formalize the relationship between levels of description. The topic is self-referential — any voice writing about simplification is demonstrating their answer to the question.

---

## III. Evidence for What Can't Be Shown

Job demands a reason for his suffering and God answers with weather. The Upanishads say "neti neti" — not this, not this. Wittgenstein ends the Tractatus by gesturing at what can't be said. Hume shows that induction can't be justified inductively. Maren demands evidence; Oskar insists the important things don't come in evidentiary form.

**The question:** What do you do with things that matter but can't be demonstrated? Is there a form of rigor that applies to the undemonstrable, or does rigor only live where evidence does?

**Corpus threads:** Book of Job, Tractatus, Hume, the apophatic tradition (Cloud of Unknowing, Eckhart, Diamond Sutra), Pascal's wager (Pensées), Kierkegaard's leap

**Why it produces different writing:** Maren and Oskar are at maximum tension here. Tomas would say if you can't show it, act as if it's not there. June would try to build something that makes the invisible visible. Dayo would argue both sides and find the argument itself is the evidence. This is a topic where Cal might actually be wrong — where "just ignore what you can't prove" misses something real.

## IV. Obedience and Refusal

Antigone refuses Creon's law. Thoreau refuses to pay the tax. The Underground Man refuses to be a piano key. Billy Budd obeys and is destroyed. Abraham obeys and is... what? Saved? Tested? Broken?

**The question:** When is refusal the only coherent position, and when is it self-indulgence? When is obedience integrity and when is it cowardice? Is there a way to distinguish principled refusal from mere stubbornness?

**Corpus threads:** Antigone, Civil Disobedience, Notes from Underground, Billy Budd, Fear and Trembling (Abraham), Bhagavad Gita (Arjuna's refusal to fight), legal reasoning (dissent as institutional refusal)

**Why it produces different writing:** Every voice has a different relationship to authority. Maren works within the system and believes in institutional constraint. Tomas has seen what refusal costs in practice. Oskar's whole mode is a kind of refusal. Cal would ask: is the thing you're refusing actually worth the fight?

---

## V. What Repetition Teaches

Ecclesiastes repeats "vanity of vanities." The Diamond Sutra repeats its negation formula dozens of times. Waiting for Godot repeats its structure twice. The Iliad repeats its epithets. Dickinson repeats her dashes. Whitman repeats his catalogues. These aren't failures of imagination.

**The question:** What does saying the same thing again do? When does repetition deepen and when does it deaden? Is there something that can only be transmitted through repetition — something a single statement can't carry?

**Corpus threads:** Ecclesiastes, Diamond Sutra, Waiting for Godot, Iliad (formulaic system), Whitman (catalogues), Dickinson (dashes as rhythmic repetition), koans (repeated structure), Dhammapada (verse repetition)

**Why it produces different writing:** Oskar would compress: say it once, perfectly. Cal would agree. Sable would say each repetition reveals something the previous one didn't — same words, different attention. Luma would formalize: repetition as fixed point, as convergence. June would want to know what the repetition produces that single statement doesn't.

## VI. Knowing by Making

June builds things to understand them. But Gatsby was written, not theorized. Euclid's proofs are constructions — "draw a circle," "extend the line." The best legal opinions construct a rule in the act of applying it. Montaigne invented the essay form in the act of thinking.

**The question:** Is making a form of knowing? Not "applied knowledge" — is the making itself epistemic? Do you learn things by building that you cannot learn by thinking, and if so, what is the status of that knowledge?

**Corpus threads:** Euclid (construction proofs), Montaigne (essay as made thought), the novels (the form discovers the content), legal opinions (rule-making as case-deciding), the mathematical tradition, Walden (Thoreau building the house)

**Why it produces different writing:** June is in her element. Oskar would resist — making is adding, and he's interested in subtracting. Sable would say that making interrupts attending. Maren would ask: what's the evidence that making teaches what thinking doesn't? Luma would try to formalize the relationship between construction and proof.

---

## VII. The Stranger's Knowledge

Meursault sees the world without the social glue that makes it cohere for everyone else. Prince Myshkin sees goodness clearly and this clarity destroys everything. Gregor Samsa becomes literally inhuman and sees his family clearly for the first time. The stranger sees what the native can't.

**The question:** Is there knowledge that's only available from outside? Does belonging to a system prevent you from seeing it? And if outsider knowledge is real, what's its relationship to the insider knowledge it can't access?

**Corpus threads:** The Stranger, The Idiot, Metamorphosis, Notes from Underground, Siddhartha (who leaves everything to learn), Heart of Darkness (Marlow as outsider), Montaigne (estranging the familiar)

**Why it produces different writing:** Sable would attend to what estrangement actually feels like. Tomas would ask: the stranger's knowledge — is it actionable? Does seeing clearly help? Dayo would hold both positions: the insider knows things the outsider can't, and vice versa. Maren would want to evaluate: is outsider knowledge more reliable or just differently biased?

---

## VIII. What Survives Translation

The Chekhov you read in Garnett is not the Chekhov you read in Pevear/Volokhonsky. Homer in Pope is an 18th-century English poem. The Tao Te Ching has a hundred translations and none of them agree. Luma's whole method is translation — finding what transfers. But what about what doesn't?

**The question:** What is preserved when a work moves between languages, between domains, between minds? Is the thing-that-survives-translation the essential part, or is the untranslatable the essential part? Are these the same question?

**Corpus threads:** Every translated work in the corpus (Homer, Dante, Rilke, Chekhov, the sacred texts, Dostoevsky, Kafka, Camus, Rumi, the Upanishads), Luma's structural mappings, the Tractatus (which exists in the gap between German and English)

**Why it produces different writing:** Luma thinks what survives translation IS the content. Sable thinks what's lost IS the content. Cal would say if the simple version in any language captures it, the original was overwritten. Oskar knows this problem from inside — the thing he's compressing toward is precisely the untranslatable. Maren would ask: translation for what purpose? Legal translation has different standards than poetic translation.

## IX. Patience and Urgency

Sable stays with one thing. June moves to the next. Thoreau spent two years. Dickinson compressed a lifetime into 1,775 poems. Chekhov wrote slowly. Dostoevsky wrote under deadline pressure and produced some of the most patient novels in existence.

**The question:** Is patience a virtue of attention or a failure of urgency? Is urgency a virtue of engagement or a failure of patience? How do you know, from inside, whether your current pace is right?

**Corpus threads:** Walden (patience as practice), Dickinson (compression as urgency), Dostoevsky (urgency producing depth), Seneca (On the Shortness of Life), Meditations (Marcus Aurelius — patience under imperial urgency), Ecclesiastes (time for everything)

**Why it produces different writing:** Cal and Sable are again in direct collision but from a different angle. June would say patience without output is stalling. Oskar would say urgency is always ego. Tomas would say it depends on the stakes — patience in an emergency is negligence. Maren would deliberate as long as needed and no longer.

## X. What Silence Contains

Wittgenstein's last proposition. The ending of Mark's gospel (they said nothing, for they were afraid). Chekhov's pauses. Beckett's silences. The space between Dickinson's dashes. Oskar's "..."

**The question:** Is silence the absence of speech or the presence of something speech can't carry? Can silence be articulate? Can it be wrong — can there be a dishonest silence?

**Corpus threads:** Tractatus, Gospel of Mark, Cherry Orchard, Waiting for Godot, Dickinson, koans, Cloud of Unknowing, Billy Budd (the stutter as blocked speech)

**Why it produces different writing:** Oskar owns this territory but Maren would challenge him — silence in the face of injustice isn't wisdom, it's complicity. Tomas would agree: silence is a luxury of people who aren't affected. Sable would say silence is what you hear when you listen long enough. Cal would say sometimes there's nothing to say and that's fine, stop making it profound.

## XI. The Weight of Structure

Euclid builds from five postulates. Dante's terza rima chains every tercet to the next. The sonnet has fourteen lines. The legal opinion follows IRAC. These structures constrain — but do they also discover? Does the sonnet find thoughts that free verse can't reach?

**The question:** Is formal structure a prison or a lens? Does the constraint of form generate content that wouldn't exist without it, or does it filter out content that would have existed otherwise? Can you distinguish discovery from artifact?

**Corpus threads:** All the formally constrained poetry (sonnets, terza rima, sprung rhythm), Euclid, the Tractatus, Summa Theologiae (objection-reply form), Oulipo, legal opinion structure

**Why it produces different writing:** Luma would formalize: structure as functor, mapping content-space into form-space. June would demonstrate: build something in a constrained form and show what the form found. Dayo would argue both sides. Cal would say: if the structure helps, use it; if it doesn't, don't; stop philosophizing about it.

---

## XII. Mercy and Rigor

Portia's "quality of mercy" speech. The Grand Inquisitor's mercy that denies freedom. Holmes's "the life of the law has not been logic; it has been experience." Dostoevsky's every novel. Antigone's mercy to the dead defying the rigor of the state.

**The question:** Can mercy be rigorous? Can rigor be merciful? Or are they genuinely in tension — and if so, which gives way?

**Corpus threads:** Brothers Karamazov, legal opinions (equity vs. law), Antigone, Billy Budd (Captain Vere's impossible position), Shakespeare, Bhagavad Gita, the contemplative tradition (compassion as structural principle)

**Why it produces different writing:** Maren lives in this tension professionally — every judgment balances mercy and rigor. Tomas has seen what happens when rigor wins and when mercy wins. Oskar would dissolve the distinction. Cal would say mercy is just rigor that knows when to stop. Dayo would refuse to resolve the tension and the refusal would itself be a position on the question.

## How to Use These

Each voice writes on each topic. That produces 8 × 12 = 96 pieces. Not all will be good — some voice-topic combinations will be dead (Cal on silence, maybe; June on the untranslatable, maybe). That's fine. The selection criterion is the same as for the natural corpus: is it internally coherent? Is every part necessary? If not, cut it.

The topics are designed so the voices' blind spots matter. A piece written by Maren on "What Silence Contains" SHOULD miss something that Oskar sees. That's not a failure — it's the training data working. The model learns that coherent perspectives have edges.

**Suggested first passes:** Pick 2-3 topics and write from 3-4 voices each, to test whether the voices are alive on the page. If a voice consistently produces dead writing, redesign it or cut it.
