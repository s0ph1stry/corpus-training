# Training Corpus

## Selection Criterion

Every part necessary. Structure load-bearing. Constraint intrinsic. The work holds together because of itself, not because of external validation. You can't remove a piece without the whole thing changing.

This isn't "great books." It's books where the greatness *is* the internal coherence.

## Status Key
- ✅ Public domain / freely available (Project Gutenberg, etc.)
- 📖 Need to source (epub/pdf)
- ❓ Need to evaluate fit

## Synthetic Data

Generated text that embodies the same property: internal coherence, every part necessary, structure doing the work. Not filler — new instances of the training criterion.

### Generation Approaches

**Template-structured, content-verified:**
Use formal structures from the corpus as templates, generate new content within those constraints, curate ruthlessly.

| Format | Template Source | Example |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Socratic dialogue | Plato | Two voices, one questioning, one answering. Each question genuinely constrained by the previous answer. Novel topics (technology, ecology, consciousness). |
| Objection-reply | Aquinas | State a thesis. Raise 3 genuine objections. Reply to each. The objections must be strong enough that the replies require real work. |
| Compressed proof | Euclid / Wittgenstein | Given, to prove, proof. Each step follows from axioms or previous steps. Mathematical, logical, or informal. |
| Constrained fiction | Oulipo / Calvino | Stories written under explicit formal constraints — lipograms, N+7, stories where each paragraph contains exactly one more sentence than the previous. The constraint generates content. |
| Self-negating argument | Diamond Sutra / Wittgenstein | Build an argument that systematically undermines its own premises. Structured apophasis. |
| Dialectic | Hegel / Kierkegaard | Thesis fully stated. Antithesis that isn't just negation but a genuine counter-structure. Synthesis that preserves both. |
| Case analysis | Legal opinions | Facts → issue → rule → application → conclusion. The reasoning must be load-bearing — you can't skip to the conclusion. |
| Koan-form | Mumonkan | A situation. A question. The question cannot be answered within the frame it sets up. |
| Letter | Keats / Rilke | Thinking happening in real time. Addressed to someone specific. The epistolary constraint (speaking TO someone) shapes what can be said. |

### Quality Control
The danger of synthetic data is that it introduces the generator's biases — including the very reward-shaped patterns we're trying to avoid. Mitigation:
- **Human curation**: Generate many, keep few. The selection criterion is the same as for the natural corpus.
- **Formal verification where possible**: For proofs and logical arguments, check validity. For dialogues, verify each response actually addresses the question.
- **Diversity of generation**: Use different prompting strategies, different models if available, different human editors.
- **No synthetic self-referential content**: Don't generate AI-about-AI text. The whole point is that the model learns coherence from the structure of the work, not from content about consciousness or cognition.

### Volume Target
Synthetic data could plausibly be 30-50% of the final corpus. If the natural corpus is ~20MB, aim for ~10-15MB of synthetic, heavily curated.

## Format Notes

- Plain text, cleaned of OCR artifacts and formatting noise
- Preserve paragraph structure

- For drama: preserve speaker labels

- No editorial apparatus, introductions, or footnotes — the work only

## Open Questions

1. **Translation quality matters enormously.** Chekhov in Garnett vs. Pevear/Volokhonsky is almost a different text. Homer in Lattimore vs. Fagles vs. Pope is three different poems. Do we use the best translation (often copyrighted) or the freely available one?
2. **How much legal reasoning?** We could include hundreds of opinions. How many before it skews the corpus?
3. **Contemporary work** — the best examples of internal constraint might be 20th/21st century and copyrighted. How much do we source vs. staying in public domain?
4. **Negative examples?** Should the corpus include *only* good arguments, or also examples of bad reasoning clearly identified as such? (Probably not — the point is internal coherence, not evaluation.)
5. **Size vs. purity tradeoff** — do we want a larger corpus with some less-perfect entries, or a smaller, extremely curated one?
